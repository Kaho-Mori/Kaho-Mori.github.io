summary: 'We present evidence that episodic mixed layer deepening drives rapid shifts in bacterioplankton community composition at a subtropical time-series station in the western North Pacific.'

abstract: |
  Short-term physical perturbations, including mixed layer deepening events, can rapidly alter the composition and metabolic state of marine microbial communities. However, the timescales and mechanisms of these responses remain poorly constrained. Here, we present results from a 72-hour Lagrangian time-series conducted at the KEO observational buoy station (32.5°N, 144.5°E) in the western subtropical North Pacific during February 2024.

  Water samples were collected every 3 hours across a depth range of 0–300 m and analyzed using 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing, flow cytometry, and dissolved inorganic nutrient measurements. A rapid mixed layer deepening event (from 100 m to 150 m within 3 hours) introduced nutrient-rich water into the lower euphotic zone, triggering measurable shifts in community structure within 6 hours. SAR11 relative abundance decreased while opportunistic copiotrophs, including Alteromonadales, increased. These findings highlight the importance of high-frequency sampling for capturing microbial responses to episodic physical forcing in oligotrophic ocean regions.
