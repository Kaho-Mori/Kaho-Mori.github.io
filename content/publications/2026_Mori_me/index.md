abstract: |
  Artificial seawater-based aquaculture systems are widely used not only for keeping ornamental fish but also for various commercial and societal purposes. A central goal of these systems is to provide a habitable environment for aquatic organisms by maintaining salinity, temperature, and pH close to natural conditions. Although microbial communities play critical roles in sustaining water quality and fish health, their long-term development in newly established tanks remains poorly understood. In this study, we monitored a newly set-up fish tank filled with artificial seawater, without transplantation of natural bacterial communities, over a four-month period, generating high-frequency time-series data on water quality and microbial community dynamics. Amplicon sequencing revealed that microbial diversity increased progressively over time, driven primarily by temporal turnover of amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) rather than simple species accumulation. Nitrogen cycling became established at the system level during the late phase, but nitrifying bacteria remained only a minor component of the water-column community, suggesting that key functional groups were not fully represented in planktonic samples. Together, these results show that diverse microbial communities can assemble during aquarium startup without transplantation of natural bacterial communities from established systems, while also highlighting a disconnect between water-column community composition and ecosystem function.
